TEST 4: WHY p=2 is special
========================================================================

  The model uses complex amplitudes propagated by linear operations.
  Linear operations on complex amplitudes that preserve reversibility
  (unitarity) form the group U(n).

  The ONLY p-norm preserved by ALL unitary transformations is p=2.
  This is a theorem (Wigner's theorem / unitarity implies Born rule).

  Therefore:
  - The model ASSUMES: complex amplitudes + linear propagation + reversibility
  - It DERIVES: |ψ|² probability (Born rule)
  - The Born rule is NOT an additional assumption — it's a CONSEQUENCE
    of the amplitude propagation rules

  BUT (the reviewer's deeper point):
  - WHY complex amplitudes? (vs real, quaternionic, etc.)
  - WHY linear propagation? (vs nonlinear)
  - WHY reversibility? (vs dissipative)
  These ARE the remaining assumptions that the model doesn't derive.

========================================================================
TEST 5: What changes if amplitudes were real (not complex)?
========================================================================

  With real amplitudes, the mixing group is O(n) instead of U(n).
  The 2-norm is still the unique preserved norm (same theorem).
  But: real amplitudes can't produce a PHASE SHIFT.
  The model's interference depends on complex phase accumulation
  (e^(i*k*action)). Without complex amplitudes:
  - No phase accumulation → no interference fringes
  - The two-slit distribution would be flat

  Verification: real-only amplitude vs complex:
  (Using the two-slit center detector at phase=0 and phase=π)

  Complex: A_up(π) + A_low = 1.2246467991473532e-16j
  → |sum|² = 0.000000 (destructive)

  Complex: A_up(π/2) + A_low = (1+1j)
  → |sum|² = 2.000000 (partial)

  Real only: can't produce phase=π/2. Limited to phase = 0 or π.
  Complex amplitudes enable CONTINUOUS phase control = richer interference.
