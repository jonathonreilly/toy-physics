1. Phase-valley saturation law

Reduced model:
  Split the detector-bound path sum into two coarse continuation valleys, v in {up, down}, with
    A_v(b) ~= W_v(b) * exp(i phi_v(b)) * exp(-sigma_v(b)^2 / 2)
  where phi_v is the valley-mean phase/action shift and sigma_v^2 is the within-valley phase spread after local path averaging.

  If the signed field bias enters mainly through the valley means while the many near-degenerate paths inside each valley self-average, the coarse kick is controlled less by raw action contrast and more by the normalized valley bias:

    Q_sat(b) = |mu_up(b) - mu_down(b)| / sqrt(sigma_up(b)^2 + sigma_down(b)^2)

  with an effective threshold-like response

    Delta_k_y(b) ~= Delta_k_sat * tanh(C * Q_sat(b))

Interpretation: the raw field/action contrast can still vary with impact parameter, but once Q_sat is O(1) or larger, the path sum has already chosen a valley sign. Further growth in the raw contrast changes the kick weakly, so the response looks like a plateau or threshold instead of a clean power law.

Retained diagnostic observable:
  valley saturation ratio
    Q_sat = signed valley-mean action gap / pooled valley action spread
  Unsaturated regime: Q_sat << 1  -> near-linear response
  Saturating regime: Q_sat >= 1   -> threshold/plateau kick

2. Why fixed-bin environments wrong-scale

  The growing-DAG sweeps now suggest one shared failure mode: branch-distinguishing structure gets projected back into a tiny common env label set.
  In mechanism language, the upper and lower slit branches increasingly land on the same effective env support, so the partial trace stops separating them.

3. Scalable environment law candidates

  Candidate A: multi-local region records
    E[path] = tensor product over local mass-region cells of small local records.
    Why it may scale: branch overlap can shrink as more cells differ, instead of collapsing into one global bin.

  Candidate B: edge/angle-sector records
    Each local cell records a coarse signed histogram of incoming/outgoing edge sectors.
    Why it may scale: different branch geometries can stay distinguishable even when they hit the same node set.

  Candidate C: path-history histogram / sequence records
    Each local region stores a short history sketch or count sketch of recent mass-region traversals.
    Why it may scale: order information survives, so same-node reuse does not automatically collapse branches together.

  Candidate D: genuinely continuous environment field
    Replace the finite-bin env label by a continuous local record variable coupled to traversal.
    Why it may scale: branch overlap can decay smoothly with record distance instead of saturating at a tiny discrete alphabet.

4. Recommended next implementation target

  Keep the unitary/path-sum core fixed and replace only the env law with a factorized local sector-record model.

  Toy law:
    E[path] = tensor product over regions r of |h_r[path]>
    h_r[path] = coarse signed histogram of angle-sector or edge-sector visits

  Then branch overlap is no longer forced through one global bin:

    <E[a]|E[b]> ~= product over r of exp(-lambda * ||h_r[a] - h_r[b]||_1)

  This is the scaling property we want: if the two branches differ over more local regions as the graph grows, the env overlap stays small or shrinks, instead of washing out.

Bottom line:
  The unitary core should stay fixed for now. The next theory move is to derive/measure Q_sat on the gravity side and replace finite-bin env labels with a local record law whose capacity grows with branch diversity.
