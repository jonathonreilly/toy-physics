## Statement

Let `W` be any nonempty finite index set, and let `sigma : W -> W` be
any involution (`sigma^2 = id_W`). Identify `sigma` with the
permutation matrix on `R^W` whose entries satisfy
`sigma_{a, b} = 1` iff `b = sigma(a)`. Hence `sigma^T = sigma` and
`sigma^2 = I`.

Let `D = diag(d_w)_{w in W}` be a diagonal matrix on `R^W` with strictly
positive entries `d_w > 0` and assume `D` is sigma-invariant in the
sense `d_{sigma(w)} = d_w` for all `w in W` (equivalently
`sigma D sigma = D`).

Let `M_+, M_-` be any pair of matrices on `R^W` whose entries lie in
the nonnegative integers `Z_{>= 0}` and which satisfy the involution
conjugacy

```text
sigma M_+ sigma = M_-.                                                    (1)
```

Define the symmetric tensor word

```text
Word(D, M_+, M_-) := D (M_+ + M_-) D (M_+ + M_-)^T D.                     (2)
```

**Conclusion (T1) (entry-wise nonnegativity).** Every entry of
`Word(D, M_+, M_-)` is a nonnegative real number,

```text
Word(D, M_+, M_-)_{a, b} >= 0  for all (a, b) in W x W.                   (3)
```

**Conclusion (T2) (sigma-equivariance).** The tensor word is
sigma-equivariant,

```text
sigma * Word(D, M_+, M_-) * sigma = Word(D, M_+, M_-).                    (4)
```

**Conclusion (T3) (image of a sigma-invariant nonnegative vector).** Let
`eta in R^W` be any vector with nonnegative entries `eta_w >= 0` that
is sigma-invariant in the sense `eta_{sigma(w)} = eta_w`
(equivalently `sigma eta = eta`). Then the image
`y := Word(D, M_+, M_-) * eta` has nonnegative entries and is
sigma-invariant,

```text
y_w >= 0  for all w in W,    and    sigma y = y.                          (5)
```

## Proof

`(T1)` Entry-wise nonnegativity.

Since `M_+` and `M_-` have entries in `Z_{>= 0}`, the sum `M_+ + M_-`
is also entry-wise nonnegative. The transpose `(M_+ + M_-)^T` is
likewise entry-wise nonnegative. The diagonal `D` has strictly
positive entries. The product of three entry-wise nonnegative matrices
is entry-wise nonnegative because each entry of the product is a sum
of products of nonnegative reals (matrix product of nonnegative
matrices is closed under multiplication). Hence every entry of
`D (M_+ + M_-) D (M_+ + M_-)^T D` is `>= 0`.

`(T2)` Sigma-equivariance.

Compute `sigma Word sigma`. Using `sigma^2 = I` and the conjugacy
hypothesis (1):

```text
sigma * (M_+ + M_-) * sigma  =  sigma M_+ sigma + sigma M_- sigma
                              =  M_- + M_+
                              =  M_+ + M_-,
```

since `sigma M_+ sigma = M_-` together with `sigma^2 = id` gives
`sigma M_- sigma = sigma (sigma M_+ sigma) sigma = M_+`. The sum `M_+
+ M_-` is therefore sigma-symmetric. Likewise `sigma D sigma = D` by
the sigma-invariance hypothesis on `D`. The transpose
`sigma (M_+ + M_-)^T sigma = (sigma (M_+ + M_-) sigma)^T = (M_+ + M_-)^T`
is also sigma-symmetric (using `sigma^T = sigma`).

Inserting `sigma sigma = I` between every factor of (2) gives

```text
sigma * Word * sigma
  = sigma D sigma * sigma (M_+ + M_-) sigma * sigma D sigma
       * sigma (M_+ + M_-)^T sigma * sigma D sigma
  = D * (M_+ + M_-) * D * (M_+ + M_-)^T * D
  = Word.
```

`(T3)` Image of a sigma-invariant nonnegative vector.

Nonnegativity of `y` follows from (T1) and the fact that `y_w =
sum_b Word_{w, b} eta_b` is a sum of nonnegative terms.
Sigma-invariance follows from (T2): `sigma y = sigma Word eta = Word
sigma eta = Word eta = y`. ∎
