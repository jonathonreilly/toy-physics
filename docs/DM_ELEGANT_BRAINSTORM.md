## The Problem

R = Omega_DM / Omega_b = 5.48 is derived with structural inputs (taste Casimir,
alpha_s from plaquette, Sommerfeld from lattice Green's function, g_* from taste
spectrum). The remaining imports are:

1. **eta** (baryon-to-photon ratio) from baryogenesis -- transport coefficients
   (v_w, L_w*T, D_q*T) are framework-derived to O(1) but not exact.
2. **The Boltzmann/Friedmann freeze-out framework itself** -- derived as the
   thermodynamic limit of the lattice master equation, but Codex sees this as
   "importing standard cosmology with framework-consistent numbers."

The compute-driven approach (derive each transport coefficient individually)
works but produces wide uncertainty bands (dR/R ~ 650% from transport corners
per DM_R_SENSITIVITY_NOTE.md). This note explores five mathematical strategies
to bypass the transport calculation entirely.

## Approach 1: The Ratio R Is Independent of eta

### Core Idea

R = Omega_DM / Omega_b. The dark sector abundance Omega_DM comes from freeze-out
of the DM species. The baryon abundance Omega_b comes from baryogenesis (eta).
But R is their RATIO. If both numerator and denominator share a common
cosmological factor, it cancels.

### Analysis

Write the explicit dependences:

    Omega_DM * h^2 = (m_DM * n_DM) / rho_crit
                   = (m_DM * s_0 * Y_DM) / rho_crit

where Y_DM = n_DM / s is the yield (number per entropy), which is set by
freeze-out and depends on sigma_v, g_*, and x_F -- but NOT on eta.

    Omega_b * h^2 = 3.65e7 * eta

So:
    R = Omega_DM / Omega_b
      = [m_DM * s_0 * Y_DM / rho_crit] / [3.65e7 * eta / h^2]

The key question: does Y_DM contain a factor of eta? In the standard freeze-out
calculation, Y_DM = n_DM/s depends on the entropy density s, the DM mass m_DM,
and sigma_v. The entropy density s depends on g_* and T, both of which are
set by the radiation-dominated thermal bath. It does NOT depend on eta.

Therefore R depends on eta INVERSELY through the denominator:

    R ~ Y_DM / eta ~ (1 / sigma_v) * (1 / eta)

These are INDEPENDENT quantities: Y_DM is set by DM annihilation physics,
eta is set by baryogenesis. They do not share a common factor that cancels.

### Verdict: DOES NOT WORK in its naive form.

R genuinely depends on both sigma_v AND eta as independent inputs. The ratio
does not simplify away the baryogenesis sector.

### Salvage path

However, there IS a structural version of this idea that partially works. If we
can show that eta is ITSELF determined by the same gauge couplings that determine
sigma_v, then R becomes a function of {alpha_s, Casimir invariants, g_*} alone.
The three Sakharov conditions are already structural. The question reduces to:
can we prove that the EWPT baryogenesis integral, evaluated with structural
inputs, yields eta ~ 6e-10? This is Approach 5 in disguise.

### Feasibility: 2/10. The ratio does not cancel eta. The idea is
seductive but algebraically wrong.

## Approach 2: Detailed Balance Argument

### Core Idea

At thermal equilibrium, the ratio of any two species depends only on their
masses, spins, and chemical potentials -- not on the expansion history. If DM
and baryons were both in equilibrium at some early temperature T >> m_DM, then:

    n_DM / n_b = (g_DM / g_b) * (m_DM / m_b)^{3/2} * exp(-(m_DM - m_b)/T)
                -> g_DM / g_b   as T -> infinity

The asymmetry between DM and baryons in the late universe arises because they
DEPART from equilibrium at different times and by different mechanisms:
- DM freezes out (Boltzmann suppression when T < m_DM)
- Baryons are produced by a CP-violating out-of-equilibrium process

### Analysis

The detailed balance argument works perfectly for SYMMETRIC DM (equal DM and
anti-DM). In this case, the late-time DM abundance is set by the freeze-out
cross-section, and R depends only on:

    R = (sigma_b_ann / sigma_DM_ann) * (phase space factors)

where sigma_b_ann is the baryon "effective annihilation" cross-section that
determines the baryon yield.

But the baryon asymmetry is NOT produced by freeze-out. It is produced by
a C/CP-violating, out-of-equilibrium process. The detailed balance theorem
does not apply to the baryon sector because the baryon number is NOT set
by equilibrium thermodynamics.

The ADM (asymmetric dark matter) literature does explore models where both
sectors have a common asymmetry generated by the same out-of-equilibrium
process. In the Cl(3) framework, the Z_3 CP violation that generates the
baryon asymmetry could plausibly also generate a DM asymmetry. If the DM
asymmetry is RELATED to the baryon asymmetry by a group-theoretic factor,
then R becomes structural.

### The ADM variant

Suppose the same sphaleron process that generates the baryon asymmetry also
generates a DM-number asymmetry, with:

    n_DM - n_DM_bar   C_2(dark)
    --------------- = ---------- * (something structural)
    n_b - n_b_bar      C_2(vis)

Then R = m_DM/m_p * (this ratio), which is purely group-theoretic.

### What would need to be proved

1. That the Cl(3) framework has a conserved quantum number in the dark sector
   (analogous to baryon number).
2. That SU(2) sphalerons violate this quantum number along with baryon number,
   in a ratio determined by the gauge group structure.
3. That the DM relic abundance is set by the ASYMMETRY (not by freeze-out of
   a symmetric population).

### Verdict: INTERESTING but requires a major model-building step.

The ADM route would bypass both the Boltzmann equation AND the transport
coefficients entirely. R would follow from representation theory of the
sphaleron anomaly. But it requires the DM candidate to carry a conserved
charge whose sphaleron violation is computable, and it requires ASYMMETRIC
DM (where antiparticles annihilate away, leaving only the asymmetric
component). This is a different physical picture from the current freeze-out
narrative.

### Feasibility: 5/10. The ADM route is physically well-motivated and
would give the most elegant result. But it requires proving that the dark
sector has the right charge structure, which is a non-trivial model-building
exercise. High payoff if it works.

## Approach 3: The Boltzmann Equation IS the Lattice Master Equation

### Core Idea

The lattice Hamiltonian generates a master equation for occupation numbers.
In the thermodynamic limit, this IS the Boltzmann equation. Not an import
but a CONSEQUENCE of the axiom. Prove this as a theorem.

### What is already proved (DM_STOSSZAHLANSATZ_THEOREM_NOTE.md)

1. **Spectral gap:** M = -Delta_L + m^2 has lambda_k >= m^2 > 0 for all k.
   (EXACT.)

2. **Exponential decay of correlations:** |G(x,y)| <= C * exp(-mu * |x-y|).
   (PROVED via Combes-Thomas on the lattice.)

3. **Cluster property / factorization:** |rho_2(x,y) - rho_1(x)*rho_1(y)|
   <= 2*C^2 * exp(-2*mu*|x-y|). (PROVED via Wick identity.)

4. **Factorization error at freeze-out:** < 10^{-45000}. (DERIVED.)

So the Stosszahlansatz is a theorem. The missing piece is the formal reduction:

    lattice master equation  --(thermo limit)-->  Boltzmann equation

### What the formal theorem would say

**Theorem (Lattice-Boltzmann Reduction).** Let H be the staggered Hamiltonian
on Z^3_L with Cl(3) algebra. Let f_k(t) = <a_k^dag a_k>(t) be the occupation
number of mode k. Then f_k satisfies a discrete master equation:

    df_k/dt = sum_{k',p,p'} W(k,k'->p,p') [f_p * f_{p'} * (1 +/- f_k)(1 +/- f_{k'})
              - f_k * f_{k'} * (1 +/- f_p)(1 +/- f_{p'})]

where W is the transition rate from the lattice T-matrix:

    W(k,k'->p,p') = |<p,p'|T|k,k'>|^2 * delta(E_k + E_{k'} - E_p - E_{p'})

In the thermodynamic limit L -> infinity with fixed lattice spacing a:

(a) The discrete sum over momenta becomes an integral: sum_k -> V * integral dk/(2pi)^3.
(b) The energy conservation delta converges to the Dirac delta.
(c) The master equation converges to the continuum Boltzmann equation pointwise
    in k for any fixed physical momentum.

### What needs to be proved for the theorem

Step (a) is standard Riemann sum convergence -- provable for any smooth
integrand, which the Boltzmann collision kernel is (away from zero-measure
singularities).

Step (b) requires showing that the finite-volume energy levels become dense.
This follows from Weyl's law for the lattice Laplacian in d=3.

Step (c) requires uniform convergence of the collision integral. This is the
technically hardest step. The potential issue is that the Boltzmann collision
kernel has a Coulomb singularity (forward scattering divergence), and the
lattice regularizes this at the lattice scale. In the thermodynamic limit,
this regularization does NOT go away (a is fixed in the framework), so the
lattice collision kernel and the continuum collision kernel are literally
identical in the relevant momentum range (momenta << pi/a).

### The elegant structure

The proof has a beautiful structure:

    spectral gap     Combes-Thomas     cluster property     Stosszahlansatz
    (EXACT)    --->  (PROVED)    --->  (PROVED)       --->  (THEOREM)
                                                               |
                                                               v
    Weyl's law       Riemann sum       collision integral   Boltzmann eq.
    (STANDARD)  ---> (STANDARD)  --->  (BOUNDED)      --->  (THEOREM)

The top row (molecular chaos) is already a theorem. The bottom row
(continuum limit of the collision integral) needs: (i) Weyl's law for
the lattice spectrum (standard), (ii) Riemann sum convergence (standard),
(iii) uniform convergence of the collision integral (the one genuinely
new mathematical step).

### Verdict: VERY PROMISING. Most of the infrastructure is in place.

The Stosszahlansatz is proved. Weyl's law and Riemann sum convergence are
standard. The one missing step is uniform convergence of the collision
integral, which should follow from the lattice UV cutoff (the integrand is
bounded on the Brillouin zone). This is a clean mathematical problem.

### Feasibility: 8/10. High likelihood of success. The key insight is
that the lattice provides a natural UV cutoff that makes the collision
integral uniformly convergent, bypassing the infrared/collinear singularity
issues that plague the continuum Boltzmann equation. This is not a bug but
a FEATURE of the lattice-is-physical axiom.

## Approach 4: Topological Argument for R

### Core Idea

R = (3/5) * C_2(adj)/C_2(fund) * S(alpha_s) * (phase space). The Casimir ratio
is a representation-theoretic invariant. The Sommerfeld factor is the lattice
Green's function at contact. Is the FULL ratio topological in some sense?

### Analysis of each factor

**Factor 1: Mass ratio 3/5.** This is the ratio of Hamming weights on the
Cl(3) bit-string space. Hamming weight is a combinatorial invariant of the
Clifford algebra. It is discrete and "topological" in the sense that it is
invariant under continuous deformations of the lattice (it depends only on
the algebraic structure, not on the lattice spacing or geometry).

**Factor 2: Channel ratio f_vis/f_dark = 155/27.** This is a Casimir
invariant computed from the representation theory of SU(3) x SU(2). These
are representation-theoretic constants: C_2(3) = 4/3, C_2(8) = 3,
dim(adj(SU3)) = 8, C_2(SU2) = 3/4, dim(adj(SU2)) = 3. These are integers
and simple fractions determined by the Lie algebra. They are certainly
"topological" in the physicist's sense (they classify representations,
which are discrete).

**Factor 3: Sommerfeld factor S_vis = 1.592.** This is the non-trivial
one. It depends continuously on alpha_s and x_F:

    S = pi * zeta / (1 - exp(-pi * zeta)),  where zeta = C_F * alpha_s * sqrt(x_F/2)

For S to be "topological," it would need to be invariant under continuous
deformations of its inputs. It is not: S varies continuously with alpha_s.

However, there is a weaker sense in which S is structural. The Sommerfeld
factor is the ratio of the exact Coulomb wavefunction at the origin to the
free wavefunction at the origin:

    S = |psi_Coulomb(0)|^2 / |psi_free(0)|^2

On the lattice, this is the ratio of the screened to unscreened lattice
Green's function at coincident points:

    S = G_screened(0, 0) / G_free(0, 0)

Both numerator and denominator are spectral sums over the lattice Brillouin
zone. In this sense, S is a "spectral invariant" -- it depends on the spectrum
of the lattice Laplacian with and without the gauge potential.

### Toward a K-theoretic classification?

The gauge group SU(3) x SU(2) x U(1) is derived from Cl(3) acting on Z^3.
The representation content (which determines the Casimir ratios) is classified
by K-theory of the Clifford algebra bundle over the Brillouin zone torus T^3.

The Atiyah-Singer index theorem relates topological invariants (Chern classes,
K-theory classes) to spectral invariants (indices of Dirac operators). In the
lattice setting, there is a lattice index theorem (the Fermion doubling theorem
of Nielsen-Ninomiya) that counts the net chirality of fermion species on the
lattice -- and this is directly related to the taste spectrum that gives g_* = 106.75.

So the chain would be:

    K-theory of Cl(3) bundle over T^3
         |
         v
    Taste spectrum -> g_* = 106.75
    Hamming weights -> mass ratio 3/5
    Representation theory -> Casimir ratio 155/27
    Lattice index -> fermion content
         |
         v
    R = f(topological invariants, alpha_s)

The only non-topological input is alpha_s (the coupling), which enters through
the Sommerfeld factor. If alpha_s could be shown to be a topological invariant
of the Cl(3) lattice gauge theory (e.g., related to a Chern-Simons invariant
or a theta angle), then R would be fully topological.

### Verdict: BEAUTIFUL but currently out of reach.

The idea that R is "topological" is spiritually correct: most of R comes from
discrete algebraic invariants. But the Sommerfeld factor's dependence on alpha_s
breaks strict topological invariance. A K-theoretic formulation would be
mathematically striking but requires deep results connecting lattice gauge
theory to K-theory that do not currently exist.

### Feasibility: 3/10. Too ambitious for near-term closure. But the
K-theoretic framing could be a powerful organizing principle for the paper
even without a full proof, since it correctly identifies which factors are
topological and which are not.

## Approach 5: R as a Theorem About Cl(3)

### Core Idea

State and prove: "For ANY unitary QFT with gauge group derived from Cl(3)
on Z^3, the ratio of dark-to-visible freeze-out abundances is R = 5.48."
This makes R a theorem about the algebra, not a numerical prediction.

### What the theorem would say

**Theorem (Cl(3) Relic Ratio).** Let H be the staggered Hamiltonian on Z^3
with algebra Cl(3) and bare coupling g satisfying {G_mu, G_mu} = 2I (A5).
Then in the thermodynamic limit:

(i) The taste spectrum decomposes as 8 = (2,3) + (2,1) under the derived
    gauge group SU(2) x SU(3), yielding g_* = 106.75.

(ii) The Wilson mass spectrum assigns Hamming weights h in {1,...,7} to
     the taste states, with the DM candidate at h=3 and visible states
     at h=1,2. The mass ratio is m_DM/m_vis = 3/5 (EXACT).

(iii) The plaquette coupling gives alpha_s = alpha_plaq(g=1) = 0.092.

(iv) The Sommerfeld factor from the channel-weighted lattice Green's function
     ratio at contact gives S_vis = 1.592 and S_dark = 1.000.

(v) The Boltzmann equation is the thermodynamic limit of the lattice master
    equation, with the Stosszahlansatz holding as a theorem.

(vi) The freeze-out condition yields x_F ~ 25 (log-insensitive).

(vii) R = (3/5) * (f_vis/f_dark) * (S_vis/S_dark) = 5.48.

### Structural analysis

Parts (i) and (ii) are PROVED (taste decomposition and Hamming weights).
Part (iii) requires A5 (g_bare = 1; see DM_AXIOM_BOUNDARY_NOTE.md).
Part (iv) is DERIVED (lattice Green's function computation).
Part (v) is BOUNDED (Stosszahlansatz proved, full reduction bounded).
Part (vi) is DERIVED (log-insensitive, so robust).
Part (vii) is assembly.

### The missing link: eta drops out IF we phrase R correctly

Here is the key insight that connects Approach 5 to Approach 1.

The standard expression R = Omega_DM / Omega_b requires knowing both Omega_DM
and Omega_b separately. Omega_b requires eta (baryogenesis). This is the
blocker identified in DM_R_SENSITIVITY_NOTE.md.

But the Lee-Weinberg formula for the freeze-out abundance gives:

    Omega_DM * h^2 = (1.07e9 GeV^{-1}) * x_F / (sqrt(g_*) * M_Pl * sigma_v)

And the baryon abundance is:

    Omega_b * h^2 = 3.65e7 * eta

So:
    R = [1.07e9 * x_F / (sqrt(g_*) * M_Pl * sigma_v)] / [3.65e7 * eta]

This DOES depend on eta. The ratio does not simplify.

HOWEVER: There is a completely different way to phrase R that avoids eta.

Define the "structural R" not as Omega_DM/Omega_b but as:

    R_structural = (sigma_b_eff / sigma_DM) * (mass ratio) * (phase space)

where sigma_b_eff is the effective cross-section that determines the baryon
yield Y_b = n_b/s. In the standard cosmology:

    Y_b ~ eta / (7.04)     (where 7.04 = photon/entropy factor)
    Y_DM ~ 1 / (sigma_v * s_0 * ...)

The problem is that Y_b is NOT set by a cross-section in the same way as Y_DM.
Baryons are produced by a CP-violating process, not by thermal freeze-out.

### The theorem's honest scope

The honest statement of the theorem is:

> Given the axioms {A1-A5}, the dark matter freeze-out abundance Y_DM is
> uniquely determined. The dark-to-baryon ratio R further requires one
> cosmological input (eta). At the observed eta, R = 5.48. The theorem
> determines R(eta) as a function; the specific value R = 5.48 requires
> the observed eta = 6.12e-10.

This is still powerful: the FUNCTION R(eta) is derived, and R = 5.48 at the
observed eta is a non-trivial consistency check. But it is not a zero-parameter
prediction.

### The baryogenesis closure variant

If we could additionally prove that the Cl(3) framework's baryogenesis
mechanism (Z_3 CP violation + SU(2) sphalerons + EWPT) uniquely determines
eta, then the full theorem would read:

> For any QFT on Z^3 with Cl(3) algebra, R = 5.48.

This requires closing the baryogenesis calculation, which circles back to
the transport coefficients.

### Verdict: PARTIALLY ACHIEVABLE. The function R(eta) is derivable as
a theorem. The specific value R = 5.48 requires either eta from observation
or a closed baryogenesis calculation.

### Feasibility: 6/10. The "R(eta) as a theorem" version is achievable
with current results (Approach 3 completes the Boltzmann reduction).
The "R = 5.48 as a theorem" version requires baryogenesis closure,
which is the hard part.

## Recommended Strategy

### Near-term (closes the gate for the paper)

**Combine Approaches 3 and 5.**

1. Complete the lattice-Boltzmann reduction theorem (Approach 3). The
   Stosszahlansatz is already proved. The remaining step is uniform
   convergence of the collision integral in the thermodynamic limit,
   which should follow from the bounded Brillouin zone. This is a
   clean analysis problem.

2. State R(eta) as a theorem about Cl(3) (Approach 5). The theorem
   says: "Given axioms {A1-A5} and the observed baryon-to-photon
   ratio, R = 5.48." The ONE imported input is eta. Everything else
   is structural.

3. Frame honestly: the framework derives the DM sector completely
   (sigma_v, Boltzmann equation, freeze-out). The baryon sector
   requires eta. The paper's DM claim is: "the ratio of dark to
   visible matter is determined by the Cl(3) algebra up to one
   cosmological input (eta), which the framework constrains but
   does not yet uniquely determine."

### Medium-term (upgrades the gate)

**Explore Approach 2 (ADM).**

If the dark sector carries a conserved charge that is violated by
SU(2) sphalerons at a rate computable from the gauge group, then
R = m_DM/m_p * (charge-violation ratio), which is purely structural
and eliminates eta entirely. This would be the most elegant closure
and would make R a genuine zero-parameter prediction.

The key question to investigate: does the Cl(3) taste decomposition
assign a conserved quantum number to the (2,1) dark sector that is
anomalous under SU(2)?

### Long-term (paper framing)

**Use Approach 4 as organizing language.**

Even without a full K-theoretic proof, the observation that R is
"almost topological" (with the only non-topological input being
alpha_s through the Sommerfeld factor) is a powerful framing device.
The paper can note that 4 of 5 factors in R are topological
invariants of the Cl(3) algebra, with the fifth being a spectral
invariant of the lattice Laplacian.
