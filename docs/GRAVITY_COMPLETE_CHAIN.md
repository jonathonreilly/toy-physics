## Step 1: Cl(3) on Z^3 --> Staggered Hamiltonian --> Lattice Propagator

**Status: EXACT**

### What happens

The Clifford algebra Cl(3) is generated by three anticommuting elements
{Gamma_1, Gamma_2, Gamma_3} satisfying {Gamma_mu, Gamma_nu} = 2 delta_{mu nu}.
On the cubic lattice Z^3, the Kawamoto-Smit construction realizes these
generators as staggered hopping operators:

    (H_hop psi)(x) = sum_{mu=1}^{3} eta_mu(x) [psi(x + e_mu) - psi(x - e_mu)]

where eta_mu(x) = (-1)^{x_1 + ... + x_{mu-1}} are the staggered phases. The
resulting propagator is the Green's function of H_hop, computed by inverting
(or path-summing) the staggered Hamiltonian on a finite lattice with
appropriate boundary conditions.

### Why EXACT

This is a standard construction in lattice field theory (Kawamoto & Smit, 1981;
Susskind, 1977). Given Cl(3) on Z^3, the staggered Hamiltonian is determined
uniquely (up to an overall coupling constant). The propagator is defined as the
matrix inverse of the Hamiltonian operator -- a mathematical object, not an
approximation.

- Nearest-neighbor coupling (encoded in "on Z^3")

## Step 2: Self-Consistency Forces the Poisson Equation

**Status: BOUNDED**

**This is the weakest link in the chain.**

### The argument

The gravitational field phi must satisfy two requirements simultaneously:

1. The propagator evolves in the field: the action is S = L(1 - phi), so the
   field modifies the propagator's path weights.
2. The propagator's density rho = |psi|^2 sources the field: L phi = -G rho
   for some local operator L.

Self-consistency demands a fixed point: phi_* such that L phi_* = -G |psi(phi_*)|^2.
On a nearest-neighbor graph, the propagator's Green's function is the inverse
of the graph Laplacian. If the field equation uses the same Green's function
(i.e., L = nabla^2), the system is self-consistent by construction. Any other
local operator L introduces a mismatch between the propagator's response kernel
and the field equation's inversion kernel.

### Why BOUNDED (not EXACT)

The argument is supported by numerical evidence, not a closed-form proof of
uniqueness:

- **5 operators tested** (original): only Poisson produces an attractive
  potential well. Biharmonic, 1/r^2 kernel, local, and random positive-definite
  operators all produce repulsive fields.
- **21 operators in parametric family**: fractional Laplacians (-nabla^2)^alpha
  for alpha = 0.25 to 3.0. The mass exponent beta(alpha) is monotonically
  decreasing, guaranteeing a unique crossing at beta = 1 (Newtonian gravity).
  On finite lattices (N = 16), finite-size bias shifts the apparent crossing
  to alpha ~ 1.5, but the structural monotonicity holds.
- **Non-local operators diverge**: next-nearest-neighbor and exponential
  coupling operators fail to converge in self-consistent iteration.
- **Screened Poisson sweep**: only the unscreened Laplacian (mu^2 = 0) gives
  the correct 1/r decay. Any mass term produces Yukawa suppression.
- **Susceptibility correlation**: the propagator's integrated density response
  correlates at r = 0.93 with the Poisson Green's function profile.

### What would upgrade this to DERIVED

A proof that on any connected graph with nearest-neighbor coupling, the unique
local operator L for which the self-consistent iteration phi -> L^{-1}(|psi(phi)|^2)
has an attractive fixed point is L = nabla^2 (the graph Laplacian). This is
plausible but not yet proved.

### Assumptions consumed

- Self-consistency (the propagator sources the field it propagates in)
- Locality (L is a short-range operator)
- Attraction requirement (the fixed point must produce a potential well)

## Step 3: Poisson on Z^3 --> Green's Function G(r) = 1/(4 pi r)

**Status: EXACT**

### The theorem

On Z^3, the lattice Laplacian Green's function satisfies:

    G(r) = <r| (-Delta_lat)^{-1} |0> = 1/(4 pi |r|) + O(1/|r|^3)

for |r| >> 1 in lattice units. This is a theorem of lattice potential theory
(Maradudin et al. 1971, Hughes 1995). The O(1/|r|^3) correction is an
oscillatory cubic-symmetry artifact that vanishes for off-axis directions and
averages to zero over solid angles.

### Why EXACT

This is a mathematical theorem about the discrete Laplacian on Z^3, proved
via Fourier analysis of the lattice Green's function:

    G(r) = (1/(2 pi)^3) integral_{[-pi,pi]^3} e^{i k . r} / [2(3 - cos k_1 - cos k_2 - cos k_3)] d^3k

The integral converges and its large-|r| asymptotics are established by
stationary phase / saddle point methods. No framework-specific claims are
involved.

### Numerical confirmation

- On-axis: 26/26 points at r in [5, 30] agree with 1/(4 pi r) to < 3%
  (residual oscillation from cubic symmetry, decaying as 1/r^3)
- Off-axis: 5/5 points agree with 1/(4 pi |r|) to < 0.5%
- Error envelope decays from 1.5% average at r ~ 5-10 to 0.4% at r ~ 25-30
- Fourier and sparse-solve methods agree at interior points
- Subtracted Fourier integral on the infinite lattice: high-precision

### Assumptions consumed

None beyond the lattice Laplacian on Z^3 (already established in Steps 1-2).

## Step 4: Point Source --> phi = -GM/r --> F = GM/r^2

**Status: DERIVED**

### The derivation

A point mass M at the origin sources the Poisson equation:

    (-Delta_lat) phi = M delta(0)

By linearity, phi(r) = M G(r). Using Step 3:

    phi(r) --> M / (4 pi |r|)    for |r| >> 1

The gravitational potential is phi = -G_N M / r with G_N = 1/(4 pi) in lattice
units (the sign convention depends on whether phi represents the potential or
the field strength; the physical content is a 1/r attractive well).

The force on a test particle is the gradient of the potential:

    F = -grad phi = G_N M / r^2    (radially inward)

### Why DERIVED (not EXACT)

The 1/(4 pi r) form is exact (Step 3), but identifying this with the
gravitational potential requires:

1. The Poisson equation holds (Step 2, BOUNDED).
2. The source is a localized delta function (point mass idealization).
3. The gradient of phi is the force (from the action S = L(1 - phi)).

Items 2 and 3 follow from the action principle and are not additional
assumptions. The DERIVED status comes from the dependence on Step 2 (Poisson).

## Step 5: Two Sources --> F = G M_1 M_2 / r^2 (Product Law)

    (-Delta_lat) phi = M_1 delta(r_1) + M_2 delta(r_2)

implies

    phi = M_1 G(r - r_1) + M_2 G(r - r_2)

The force on source 2 due to source 1 is:

    F_12 = -M_2 grad phi_1(r_2) = M_1 M_2 / (4 pi |r_1 - r_2|^2)

The product M_1 M_2 is NOT imposed as a bilinear ansatz V(x_1, x_2) = s_1 s_2 / r.

## Step 6: The Exponent 2 = d - 1 = 3 - 1

**Status: EXACT**

In d spatial dimensions, the Poisson equation (-Delta) phi = rho has a Green's
function with the asymptotic form:

    G_d(r) ~ 1 / r^{d-2}    for d >= 3

The force is the gradient:

    F = -grad phi ~ 1 / r^{d-1}

For d = 3: F ~ 1/r^2. The exponent 2 is exactly d - 1.

The spatial dimension d = 3 is itself derived from Cl(3): the Clifford algebra
Cl(3) has exactly 3 generators, and the lattice Z^3 has exactly 3 spatial
dimensions. Two independent hard bounds confirm uniqueness:

1. **Lower bound d >= 3**: At d = 1 and d = 2, the Poisson Green's function

The intersection is d = 3 uniquely.

### Why EXACT

selection arguments are mathematical bounds (proved for the hydrogen atom in

## The Weakest Link: Step 2

### Why it matters

If Step 2 were EXACT, the entire chain from Cl(3) to F = GM_1 M_2 / r^2 would
be a mathematical theorem. Instead, the Poisson equation's status rests on
numerical evidence across 21 operators on finite lattices (up to N = 24 for
self-consistency iteration, N = 16 for parametric sweep).

### What the evidence shows

The evidence is strong but not a proof:

1. **Sign discrimination**: among all tested operators, only Poisson produces
   an attractive gravitational well. This is a qualitative, binary test --
   attractive vs repulsive -- with no ambiguity in the outcome.

2. **Monotonic uniqueness**: the mass exponent beta(alpha) for the fractional
   Laplacian family is monotonically decreasing in alpha, guaranteeing a unique
   crossing at beta = 1. The crossing point is alpha = 1 (standard Poisson)
   in the continuum limit.

3. **Connectivity matching**: non-local operators (NNN, long-range) diverge
   in self-consistent iteration. Only operators matching the propagator's own
   nearest-neighbor connectivity converge.

### What would close it

A proof that on any connected graph with nearest-neighbor coupling, the
graph Laplacian is the unique local operator whose inverse (applied as a
field equation kernel) produces an attractive self-consistent fixed point
with the propagator density. This is a well-posed mathematical question.

## What Is NOT in This Chain

The following are NOT claimed as part of the gravity derivation:

1. **Strong-field GR**: horizons, frame dragging, gravitational waves beyond
   linearized regime. The action S = L(1 - phi) is valid for phi << 1 only.

2. **The gravitational constant G_N in SI units**: the chain gives G_N = 1/(4 pi)
   in lattice units. Converting to SI requires identifying the lattice spacing
   with a physical length (one calibration from observation).

3. **Full Einstein equations**: only the weak-field, static, spherically
   symmetric sector is derived. The dynamic sector (gravitational waves via
   the wave equation promotion nabla^2 -> Box) is separately documented.

4. **The spatial metric factor of 2**: the GR light bending factor is
   consistent with the framework but conditional on deriving the spatial
   metric contribution from the propagator structure.
