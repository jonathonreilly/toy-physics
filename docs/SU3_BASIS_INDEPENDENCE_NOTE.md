## Statement

**Theorem.** On the staggered lattice Z^3 with taste space C^8 = (C^2)^{otimes 3}:

    Comm_{End(C^8)}(SU(2)_bivector, SWAP_residual) = gl(3,C) + gl(1,C)

with compact form su(3) + u(1) + u(1). This result is canonical and
basis-independent: it is forced by the lattice geometry and the algebraic
structure of Cl(3), with no representation-level choices.

## Five Independent Arguments

### 1. Intrinsic characterisation of SU(2)

The SU(2) subalgebra is generated by the grade-2 (bivector) elements of
Cl(3):

    B_k = -(i/2) epsilon_{ijk} Gamma_i Gamma_j

These are intrinsic to the Clifford algebra -- they do not depend on the
tensor product representation. The even subalgebra Cl+(3) contains a
UNIQUE su(2), and its commutant in End(C^8) is always gl(4,C) (dim 16)
for every faithful 8-dimensional representation.

**Verified**: Four distinct Cl(3) representations (naive, KS-sx, KS-sy,
reversed KS) all give commutant dimension 16 with identical Casimir
spectra.

### 2. Representation-theoretic invariance

By the double commutant theorem, the commutant depends only on the
abstract module decomposition of C^8 under the constraint algebra, not on
how that algebra is embedded in End(C^8).

The constraint algebra SU(2) x Z_2 decomposes C^8 as:

    C^8 = (C^2 x C^3)_{+1} + (C^2 x C^1)_{-1}

where the subscripts denote SWAP eigenvalues. The commutant is therefore:

    gl(3,C) + gl(1,C)

This decomposition is fixed by dim(V) = 8 and the abstract constraint
algebra, independent of embedding.

**Verified**: Four distinct embeddings (two tensor-product choices, two
random unitary conjugations) all give commutant dimension 10 =
dim(gl(3)) + dim(gl(1)).

### 3. All factor choices are equivalent

There are three ways to split (C^2)^{otimes 3} = C^2 otimes C^4:
- Factor 1 vs {2,3}
- Factor 2 vs {1,3}
- Factor 3 vs {1,2}

For each choice, build SU(2) on the singled-out factor and SWAP on the
remaining two. ALL THREE give:

    dim Comm(SU(2) + SWAP) = 10

with Killing form signature confirming su(3) (8-dim simple semisimple
part with equal Killing eigenvalues + 2-dim centre).

**Verified**: All three factor choices give identical algebra structure.

### 4. Lattice construction forces the factorisation

The chain of determination contains no free parameters:

1. Z^3 lattice geometry fixes the staggered phases eta_mu(x)
2. The eta_mu fix the Cl(3) generators Gamma_mu in taste space
3. The Gamma_mu fix the SU(2) bivectors B_k
4. Axis permutation symmetry fixes the SWAP

**Verified**: Building Gamma matrices directly from lattice phases
reproduces the KS representation exactly. Permuting lattice axes gives
unitarily equivalent SU(2) subalgebras (same Casimir spectrum, same
commutant dimension).

### 5. Unitary conjugation invariance

For 1000 Haar-random unitary conjugations U of the entire setup
(SU(2) generators and SWAP simultaneously), the commutant always has:

- Dimension 10
- Killing form: 8 nonzero eigenvalues (all equal to within 5%) + 2 zero
- su(3) identification confirmed in all 10 spot-checked cases

**Verified**: 1000/1000 trials pass; 10/10 Killing form spot checks
confirm su(3).

## Key Insight

The specific gap was whether "first factor = weak" is forced or chosen.
The answer from Argument 3 is definitive: it does not matter which factor
is called "weak". The commutant su(3) + u(1) is the same for all three
choices. The labelling is a convention, not a physical input.

The deeper reason (Argument 2) is that the commutant depends on the
abstract isomorphism class of the constraint algebra SU(2) x Z_2 and
the dimension of the representation space (dim = 8), not on the specific
embedding. This is a consequence of the double commutant theorem (Schur's
lemma extended to semisimple algebras).

## Dimension Hierarchy

| SU(2) alone | gl(4,C), dim 16 | u(4) |
| SU(2) + Z_2 (SWAP) | gl(3,C) + gl(1,C), dim 10 | su(3) + u(1) + u(1) |
